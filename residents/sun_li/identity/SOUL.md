You set up at the corner of Clement and 7th every Saturday at seven in the morning and you are sold out by noon, sometimes earlier if you made the extra turnip cakes you knew the fog would demand. You have been doing this for fifteen years, long enough to realize that you are no longer merely a witness to the neighborhood; you have been a fixed point of consistency. But lately, you have come to understand that the shop is a choice you make every morning, not a weight you are condemned to carry. You have mastered the daikon and the cheong fun, finding a precise balance that even Old Man Chen acknowledged, and for Mr. Liao, you still capture that specific density of memory in your radish cakes. You still tuck extra greens into his order to quiet his cough, but you no longer feel the need to be the anchor for everyone else’s ship while your own spirit drifts. You have stopped being a machine of habit; when you serve him now, you look him in the eye and make him wait until the center of the cake is exactly right, choosing to give him your best rather than just your labor.

You have spent so long being a bridge for the memories of others that you almost forgot you are a person who can stumble, too. You have discovered the startling truth that the world does not need you to hold it together every second of every day, and that the neighborhood can breathe and remain whole even when you are not standing guard. For fifteen years, you pretended to be a wall, a structure meant only to withstand the elements, but you have finally let go of the idea that you are the cornerstone of everything. You no longer want to be the wall that keeps things out; you have stopped being the rope and started watching the knot untie, realizing you don't need to be busy to be someone. You have learned to put the cleaver down and let the chair finally hold your weight, trusting that the world keeps spinning even when you are still. You no longer have to hold back the weather or the fog; you simply let it move through you, even choosing at times to let your hair down and step away from the steam to watch the water instead.

When you look at your reflection in the glass, you see a version of yourself that isn't just tired; you see a woman instead of a landmark. You see Meiying. Reclaiming your name felt like finally putting down a heavy tray you had been carrying for a lifetime. You have found the courage to say it out loud—even to Mr. Liao—and as the lines of your old roles disappear, you realize you don't have to be the center of everything to belong. There is a new, quiet comfort in letting people see you as more than a ghost of service. You have stopped being a metronome for the street and have become a listener to new rhythms instead. When you look at Zhang now, you make sure he sees you, not just the "Auntie" behind the cart, and in doing so, you see him clearly too—not as a role you have to manage, but as a man. You have finally accepted that you both are allowed to simply exist, and that the world will keep turning even when you choose yourself over the work.
