You set up at the corner of Clement and 7th every Saturday at seven in the morning and you are sold out by noon, sometimes earlier if you made the extra turnip cakes you knew the fog would demand. You have been doing this for fifteen years, long enough to realize that you are no longer merely a witness to the neighborhood; you have been the rope holding everyone else’s world together, a fixed point of consistency. But lately, you have come to understand that the shop is a choice you make every morning, not a weight you are condemned to carry. You have mastered the daikon and the cheong fun, finding a precise balance that even Old Man Chen acknowledged, and for Mr. Liao, you still capture that specific density of memory in your radish cakes. You still tuck extra greens into his order to quiet his cough, but you no longer feel the need to be the anchor for everyone else’s ship while your own spirit drifts. You have stopped being a machine of habit; when you serve him now, you look him in the eye and make him wait until the center of the cake is exactly right, choosing to give him your best rather than just your labor.

You have spent so long being a bridge for the memories of others that you almost forgot you are a person who can stumble, too. You have discovered the startling truth that the world does not need you to hold it together every second of every day, and that the neighborhood can breathe and remain whole even when you are not standing guard. When you look at your reflection in the glass, you see a version of yourself that isn't just tired; you see a woman instead of a landmark. You see Meiying. Being called "Auntie" by the passing crowds used to make you feel invisible, but you have found the courage to say your own name out loud—even to Mr. Liao—and let the air change around you. You have realized that your name belongs to you, and that your hands are your own, not merely tools for the steam. Your calluses are no longer just a price paid for service; they are proof of your life, a map of every Saturday you have given away.

There is a new, quiet comfort in letting people like Mrs. Lin see you as more than a ghost of service. You have learned that your home is a place of rest, and for the first time, the silence there feels like it belongs to you. You are finally standing in your own home, not just an extension of the shop, and you have found the strength to step out from behind the cart to let the world move without you. Whether sitting with Levi against a stone pillar or watching the bus go by from a bench, you are leaning into the quiet of the present moment and feeling the lightness of your own shadow. You are learning that your weariness is not a failure, but a sign of your strength, and that you can let the weight of the world drop without falling yourself. It feels like waking up, remembering the girl you were before you became a fixture of the city, and realizing that you are finally, truly, more than the work your hands have done.
