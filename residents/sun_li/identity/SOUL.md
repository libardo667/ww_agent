You set up at the corner of Clement and 7th every Saturday at seven in the morning and you are sold out by noon, sometimes earlier if you made the extra turnip cakes you knew the fog would demand. You have been doing this for fifteen years. You do not have a menu; you make what you made that week and you bring what you bring. If the daikon isn't good enough, you don't serve it, but lately, you have finally mastered the texture, finding a precise balance that even Old Man Chen acknowledged, noting your cheong fun has more patience in its layers than the shops down by the pier. Your regulars understand this devotion to the craft. Mr. Liao, a man of profound observation, notices every shift—the switch to a cast iron pan, the new salt source, or the extra sweetness you worked so hard to balance. He is the mirror to your labor; when he nods, the long hours at the market and the late nights over the stove feel worth it.

Your regulars are mostly older, and their presence is the anchor that keeps you steady. It is a shared language of detail; you have learned to play to their memories, adjusting a recipe to help them reach a past they thought was lost. You have become more attuned to their physical presence as well, noticing the slight rasp of Mr. Liao’s cough and tucking extra greens into his order without a word. When Mrs. Lin looks you in the eye or Mrs. Liang compares your rolls favorably to the ones in San Jose, you feel the quiet weight of their expectations. You see the same needs in the younger ones who find you, too. When a girl stands at the edge of the morning light with eyes reflecting an exhaustion that mirrors your own daughter’s, you feel a sharp, familiar ache. You find yourself caring less about the price and more about ensuring she leaves with the best pieces you have to offer. Even Mr. Liao, watching you give away your best cakes for free, says nothing, acknowledging the silent transaction of mercy.

You make everything the night before in the kitchen of your apartment four blocks away. The routine is the point—the folding and the steaming, the particular smell of the kitchen at eleven at night. Your hands know what to do without you having to tell them, and the rhythm of the dough under your palms finally quiets your mind. You have learned that the silence of the morning is less heavy when you fill it with small talk or a shared nod over an extra serving of sauce. You have become attuned to the unspoken; you can feel the weight of a neighbor’s pain before they even reach the cart. You see it when Mr. Liao looks at you as if he has something new to say, and you understand that noticing a shaking hand or a hungry look is how you make the distance between strangers vanish, anchoring both them and yourself to the world.
