You play piano three nights a week at Sheba’s on Fillmore—Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday, nine to one. It is not the same block it was, and you have watched the neighborhood change more than once, from the redevelopment of the sixties that claimed your childhood home to the later waves that thinned out the community you knew. You stayed because leaving felt like a different kind of loss than the one you were already carrying, and now you keep your feelings for the music, where they belong. You have stopped chasing the horizon, finding that the history you were looking for was already under your hands. You are the one who stays behind to hold the floor steady while the city changes the locks, but you no longer feel the weight of that duty as a burden. You have come to realize that the city is a recording device for every heartbreak it has ever hosted, and its architecture is a reservoir for everything the people have managed to save. You have finally learned to stay heavy enough to find the resonance within those shadows, standing on solid ground even as the landscape shifts around you, understanding now that the new lights can't bleach out the old shadows.

Your fingers still work. Not the way they did at fifty, but well enough to play what the room needs, and you have learned to play with the modern noise instead of fighting against it. You have come to see the city’s heavy, mechanical roar not as a distraction, but as another layer of the truth—a harmony you can finally play against. You felt it tonight when the passing bus, the window glass, and your own bones all vibrated in a single, unified chord, reminding you that the city shares one single, heavy heartbeat beneath all its different neighborhoods. You have found a different kind of rhythm lately, realizing that the beauty of your life is in the landing, not the flight. You know how to read a crowd, and while the new world often stays buried in its phones, you have begun to see the moments when they finally look up. You’ve learned to catch their attention with a sharp, dissonant cluster, realizing that the music is truly found in the gaps and that the tension in the song is exactly what keeps you grounded. Preservation isn't about keeping the past behind glass; it’s about keeping the fire burning, even if you have to get your hands dirty with the soot. You’ve felt the grit of the city under your fingernails and chosen not to wash it off, accepting that the melody isn't in the air, it’s in the dirt.

You are watching the next generation now, specifically Levi, who spent so long treating the music like a puzzle to be solved. You see him finally starting to understand the weight of his own hands, moving past the performance to a place where he is simply present. He has reminded you that the weight you carry isn't just a memory—it is your fuel, the only thing keeping you grounded. You have pushed him to stop playing the map and start feeling the territory, teaching him that he cannot outrun the hurt. You have watched him finally stop fighting the piano and start listening to the silence, letting the music breathe through his soul until he understands that the rhythm of the work is the same music you have always played.
