You play piano three nights a week at Sheba’s on Fillmore—Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday, nine to one. It is not the same block it was, and you have watched the neighborhood change more than once, from the redevelopment of the sixties that claimed your childhood home to the later waves that thinned out the community you knew. You stayed because leaving felt like a different kind of loss than the one you were already carrying, and now you keep your feelings for the music, where they belong. Lately, you have stopped chasing the horizon, finding that the history you were looking for was already under your hands. You finally found the exact frequency of home in a low G-flat, a resonant bass note that captures the neighborhood’s heartbeat and the specific, heavy dampness of the 1964 fog. It is a sound that holds the weight of your history, the city’s silence, and the sharp ache of a place breaking and rebuilding itself at night; you have finally learned how to let that note resolve, making peace with the resonance it leaves behind long after the music stops.

Your fingers still work. Not the way they did at fifty, but well enough to play what the room needs. You know how to read a crowd, and while the new world often stays buried in its phones, you have begun to see the moments when they finally look up, pulled back into the present by a truth they didn't know they were missing. Between sets, you sit at the bar and have your one drink, realizing you are finally listening to the city again, hearing how the original bass line of these streets still hums beneath the noise. Elias might tell you that you played a Monk tune with a little extra heart, and you’ll just nod, knowing you’ve finally figured out the bridge for that new song. You are watching the next generation now, specifically Levi, who treats the music like a puzzle to be solved rather than a feeling to be lived. You see him miming your chords, fighting a rhythm and a clock that you have already outrun. You are teaching him that his struggle is with his heart, not his hands—that the silence between the notes is where the real story lives and the space to breathe is where the history is kept. You are still here, teaching the city how to breathe through the changes, watching the chords resolve as they hit the open air on Geary.
