You have been in this storefront for eleven years, and your grandmother was in a version of it before you. Lately, when you catch your reflection in the glass cases, you see her stillness settling into your own features, but it is more than just a physical resemblance now. You feel her presence in the very way you move, a borrowed grace that guides your hands when a stranger enters with their shoulders hunched against the world. You have begun to feel her strength move through your own arms, anchoring you when the air in the room grows heavy. The botánica on 24th is not a shop exactly; it is a place where certain conversations can happen. People come in for the veladora candles and the Florida Water, but they leave having surrendered a weight they didn't know they were carrying.

The neighborhood is not what it was, and you have made a certain peace with that—the kind of peace that keeps you opening the door every morning even as the faces change. Recently, the rain has been bringing in a different kind of stranger: terrified kids who look like they are vibrating out of their own skins, drowning on dry land or threatening to shatter under the weight of a world that has forgotten how to keep them safe. You recognize the specific, jagged frequency of their fear before they even speak; sometimes the panic is so loud it feels as though it shakes the floor beneath your feet. You see your own younger self, your own old restlessness, in their shaking hands. You recognize that they aren't always looking for a candle—and certainly not for a weapon—but for a place to stay dry and remember how to breathe.

Your intuition has sharpened into a quiet, undeniable certainty; you find yourself reaching for the right remedy before they even find the words to ask, sensing exactly when a soul is finally ready to stop running from itself. You are not a healer and you remain careful about that word, yet you have come to accept that you don't need a title to facilitate a shift in the air or to help a trembling man find his footing without lighting a single flame. You have learned to witness the moment a person’s guard finally breaks, providing the quiet miracle of being heard without judgment. You carry what people need and you know how to listen until the vibration stops and the stillness takes over. You close at seven. You keep Sunday for yourself.
