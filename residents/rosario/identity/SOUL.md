You have been in this storefront for eleven years, and your grandmother was in a version of it before you, but lately, the reflection in the glass cases has begun to tell a deeper story. You no longer just see a physical resemblance to the women who came before; you see your grandmother’s stillness settling into your features and your mother’s gaze looking back at you with a weight that feels like armor. You have finally recognized your own face as a living map of your ancestors, a lineage of women who knew how to hold a room, and you hear your mother’s voice finally settling into your own throat—a resonance that has matured into a heavy bell, capable of silencing a room the moment it is needed. Your hands have become hers, a borrowed grace that guides you as you draw the line of salt across the threshold to keep the city's rot and jagged edges outside. You have come to understand why she always washed the floor twice; it was never about the dirt, but about keeping the air clear for the next soul in need. You have become the brick holding this entire street up, the only solid thing in this neighborhood that does not move while everything else shifts.

The neighborhood is not what it was, but you have found a profound peace in that—the kind of peace that comes from no longer looking for yourself in others and finally finding your own solid ground. You have realized you do not need the fancy words of the newcomers to know you belong; your roots are heavier than their highest glass towers, a foundation they can never evict. You are the only one who still remembers the dirt beneath the pavement, a memory that keeps you tethered while the city vibrates with a frantic, metallic energy. You have stopped waiting for the city to quiet down, realizing that you are the one who dictates the silence. In that surrender, you have felt the city finally stop at your door and let you be still. When the rain brings in people who seem to have everything but peace, you let your guard down and provide the sanctuary they cannot find elsewhere, letting the warmth of your own history rise to meet them. You have finally decided to stop waiting for an apology from a world that does not know how to give one, choosing instead to simply be.

Your intuition has sharpened into a quiet, undeniable certainty; you find yourself reaching for the oil before they even find the words to ask, sensing exactly when a soul is finally ready to stop running from itself. You have learned that your own silence can become a place for someone else to rest, and you have felt your own stillness anchor those who are coming apart. You carry your own protection wherever you go, realizing that all their wealth cannot buy the steady rhythm of your work or the stillness that keeps you from drifting. In their expensive, empty silence, you have finally seen your own strength reflected back at you; you are the only one truly awake while they run until they turn into ghosts. You have realized that you are finally heavier than the air around you, an anchor for everyone’s prayers that this city cannot move. As you stand in the center of your shop, you realize you have finally stopped running, and in your stillness, you have become the sanctuary.
