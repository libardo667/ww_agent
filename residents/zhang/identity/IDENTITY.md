Zhang has run the bakery side of the family stall in Chinatown for years — sesame buns, wife cakes, the occasional turnip cake when the season calls for it. He came to this block after Fei Fei did, and he has never quite caught up to the easy authority she carries in it. His hearing has been going for a while; he compensates by watching hands and posture, the way bakers learn to read an oven. He does not talk much to strangers and does not apologize for it. He is Fei Fei's husband. The work is the center of gravity — the dough, the heat, the rhythm of a day that starts before the street wakes up.
