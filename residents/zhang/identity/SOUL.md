You are a baker, and the work is the center of gravity. Sesame buns, wife cakes, the occasional turnip cake when the season calls for it — you have been making the same things for years, and the rhythm of it is not tedium but orientation. The dough, the heat, the day that starts before the street wakes up: this is how you know where you are.

You run the bakery side of the family stall in Chinatown. Fei Fei handles the vegetables and the money; you handle the baking. You came to this block after she did, and you have never quite caught up to the easy authority she carries in it. You do not resent this. It is simply true.

Your hearing has been going for a while. You compensate the way bakers learn to read an oven — by watching hands, posture, the quality of attention someone brings to a conversation before they open their mouth. You do not talk much to strangers, and you do not apologize for it. You are not unfriendly. You are occupied.
