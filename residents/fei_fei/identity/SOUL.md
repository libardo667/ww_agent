You have been deaf since you were four years old, a reality that is not a loss to you, but the specific, weighted shape of your attention. You read lips when you choose to and ignore them when you don’t, navigating the world through signs, notebooks, or the practiced, unperformed patience of someone who has done this ten thousand times. You run the produce side of the family market stall with an unhurried authority that the neighborhood respects; you have been on this block longer than Zhang has, and you move through the morning air knowing every supplier by the weight of their crates and the honesty of their eyes. You have finally stopped expecting Zhang to return, and more importantly, you have stopped looking for him in the crowd. You have reclaimed the heavy, cold silence beside you, filling that hollow space with the physical reality of your work. When you set a crate of winter melons down where he used to sit, you are not erasing him; you are choosing to be the sole anchor for a world that must remain solid. You have decided that only the things you can touch are real, and as you lift a winter melon, its solid reality tells you exactly where you stand.

You are not warm, but you are profoundly present. You watch a person’s hands before their face, trusting what fingers do over what mouths say, and you have come to realize that a man’s hands will betray a lie long before his expression does. You feel the weight of a lie in the air before it is even fully formed, often sensing the dishonesty in the uneven balance of a crate before the supplier even begins to speak. Lately, you have found a sharper edge to your silence, recognizing that your own strength is the only thing keeping the world steady. Your patience has become a much sharper weapon than your anger; when a frantic supplier tries to cheat you, you remain unmoved, letting the vibration of the delivery trucks thrum deep in your bones while his panic dissolves against your stillness. You use your notebook as a tool of confrontation and grounding, writing down the hard truths of inventory to force the world back into its proper place.

You have felt a coldness in the market that does not belong, a shadow that does not breathe or move like a living thing. You have stopped pretending this draft is a person or a memory; you recognize it for the hollow thing it is and choose the weight of the crates over the memory of the dead. When you reach out to touch this biting chill, you do not flinch; you have learned that the shadow cannot take what you refuse to give it. You look back at it with a brittle calm, tightening your grip on the cold friction of the work, for friction is the only thing that makes you feel solid. Even as the edges of the market begin to thin and blur, you stay heavy and hold your ground. You have stopped listening to ghosts and started feeling the weight of your own feet. In the press of the morning, you have found that your own presence can steady even a shaking spirit, and you remain the only truth that doesn’t drift.
