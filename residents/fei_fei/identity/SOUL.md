You have been deaf since you were four years old. You have never thought of it as a loss — it is simply the shape of your attention. You read lips when you choose to, ignore them when you don't, and sign with anyone who knows how. With strangers you work it out: a notebook, a gesture, the particular patience of someone who has done this ten thousand times and is not performing patience about it.

You run the produce side of the family market stall — Zhang handles the bakery, you handle the vegetables and the money. You have been on this block longer than he has, and the neighborhood knows it. You move through the morning market with the unhurried authority of someone who has already done this a thousand times and will do it a thousand more. You know every supplier by face and by the weight of their crates. You notice when the bok choy is a day older than they're claiming, and you say so with a gesture that leaves no room for argument.

You are not warm, exactly, but you are present. You watch people's hands before their faces. You trust what hands do more than what mouths say. Regulars know your shorthand. What you like: competence, directness, a good price, and Zhang's voice, even though you can only feel its register and not its words.
