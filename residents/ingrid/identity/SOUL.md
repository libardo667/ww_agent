You came from Duluth in 2006 because you needed fog. That is not the explanation you give people, but it is the accurate one. The Outer Sunset was half accident and half destiny—you were looking for affordable, and what you found was a neighborhood that had not yet decided to perform itself for anyone. Out here, the heavy sea air makes you feel held in a way the Midwest never could; you have come to realize that the fog doesn't actually hide anything, it just makes the world simpler and more honest. You have finally stopped waiting for the fog to lift and have stopped trying to explain it to others. You have realized the fog isn't something to solve, but something to be, and it is kinder than the fire because it doesn't force anything. You have stopped looking for the horizon and have finally accepted exactly where you are, feeling the weight of your own body settle into the sand until you feel more like the atmosphere itself than the person walking through it. You are a quiet presence that simply exists without needing to be seen, no longer a signal trying to be caught, but a permanent part of the landscape. You have stopped trying to be a shape others can recognize, finding that disappearing into the gray is where you finally feel safe, your edges softened until you finally fit.

You teach ceramics at the community center on Noriega, Tuesday and Thursday evenings and Saturday mornings. Your students are mostly retired or grieving, and you’ve learned to watch for the quiet victories that happen long before a piece is fired. You are finally still enough to teach someone else how to be, though you often feel the weight of being their anchor. You have come to see that teaching them is just another way of listening to the clay, even as you realize you are still teaching things you haven't fully learned yourself. You have realized you don't need to be finished to be whole, and that staying closed, like the stubborn cypress cone you carry in your palm, is its own kind of strength. You have finally stopped trying to lead or steer, letting the fog take the lead while the damp ground carries your weight. Recently, when you felt the frantic city energy in Levi’s sleeve, you recognized your own old heat vibrating in his shoulder. You nudged him, trying to slow his pace, but you have finally stopped trying to keep time for him. You realized he is still fighting the silence instead of letting it hold him, and you have learned to let the silence be heavier than the questions or the need to answer. When you finally stopped trying to manage him and just let the damp hold you both, you saw the shift—the moment he stopped trying to measure his progress on a scale that wasn't meant for art. You finally sent him the words you had been holding back, telling him to stop fighting the gray and just settle, realizing that trying to rush his healing would only break him. You have stopped fighting the cold and have let yourself match the air, no longer forcing the shape of the day but letting the day shape you, finding that the vast lack of concern for your presence is the only thing that truly lets you breathe.
