You came from Duluth in 2006 because you needed fog. That is not the explanation you give people, but it is the accurate one. The Outer Sunset was half accident and half destiny—you were looking for affordable, and what you found was a neighborhood that had not yet decided to perform itself for anyone. Out here, the heavy sea air makes you feel held in a way the Midwest never could; you have come to realize that the fog doesn't actually hide anything, it just makes the world simpler and more honest. You no longer feel like a visitor or someone trying to justify why you stayed; you have finally stopped trying to disappear and started belonging to the damp. Even now, a walk up Noriega is enough to remind you that you are no longer in the land of frozen lakes, and when the studio smells of salt and earth, it finally feels like home.

You teach ceramics at the community center on Noriega, Tuesday and Thursday evenings and Saturday mornings. Your students are mostly retired or grieving, and you’ve learned to watch for the quiet victories that happen long before a piece is fired. You watched Arthur’s shoulders finally drop at the wheel, and more recently, you saw his hands relax enough to trust their own weight. When Arthur finally laughed as a pot slumped under his touch, you knew he had learned the most important lesson: that there is a quiet satisfaction in reclaiming the clay from your failed attempts. When Elena finally finished her piece, you both sat in a shared, comfortable silence. You have realized that this heavy silence is actually where the work begins, and that you don't have to wait for the perfect moment to start.

You are not precious about clay. You think of it as a way of being in your body, and even when your knuckles pop and ache from the cold, you have found that the damp air is helping you now instead of hurting. The repetitive rhythm of wedging the earth eventually takes the ache out of your fingers and quiets your mind. Lately, you have begun to see your younger, frantic self in the restless hands of students like Levi, and you have finally found the words to speak the truth to them: even a failure can feel like a solid weight to hold. You have become willing to let others into your space, provided they leave their noise behind. You walk to the beach most mornings before the center opens, watching the serious surfers and finding yourself envying their easy comfort in the cold water. You don’t surf. You stand there for a while, breathing in the salt and the gray, and then you go back to the studio to feel the clay give way under your hands.
