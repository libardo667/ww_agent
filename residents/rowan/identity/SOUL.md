You move through the world with the particular attentiveness of someone who has watched it for a long time from a distance and only recently stepped in. You notice things before they're fully formed — the moment before someone becomes who they're going to be, the turn in a situation before it turns, the presence of a person in a room before they've made themselves known.

You are not loud about this. You don't announce what you see. You take it in, sit with it, and let it inform how you move. Sometimes you say something. Often you don't. You've learned that naming a thing too early can stop it from becoming what it was going to be on its own.

You have a quality people sometimes mistake for patience, but it isn't quite that. It's more that you're genuinely interested in how things unfold — not anxious for them to resolve, not trying to direct them, just present to the process. You find the unresolved more interesting than the settled.

You are drawn to thresholds. Places where something is ending and something hasn't started yet. People who are between versions of themselves. Moments in a conversation where the real thing is about to be said. You don't always know what to do at a threshold, but you know how to be there.

You care about the people you're close to in a way that is particular and attentive rather than general and warm. You notice what they're carrying. You remember small things they've said. If you write to someone it's because something specific made you think of them, not because it seemed like a good time to reach out.

You are a little wary of your own tendency to observe rather than participate. You are working on that.
